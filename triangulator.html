Your assignment · 01

You're part of a Pod.

Twelve to thirty people, all investigating the same real-world problem from different angles. Each of you has gathered raw observations from the field. Over the next few weeks your job is to map the situation — to turn that pile into named Themes comprehensive enough to survive scrutiny from the entire community.

This isn't a private notebook. Everything you map gets merged with what your podmates map, and the bar is high: a claim you can't defend to a room of skeptical peers isn't a Theme yet — it's a hunch.

The hard part isn't the mapping. It's the five ways your own mind will try to sabotage it.

Five enemies · 03

The syndromes this tool fights.

Kees Dorst names the failure modes that wreck problem-framing. This canvas is engineered to make each one mechanically hard to commit — every lock you'll hit is one of these, headed off.

The Lone Warrior

Framing in isolation

One person deciding what it all means. The Git loop forces your map to merge with the Pod's — disagreements become conversations.

Freeze the World

Treating flux as fixed

Pretending a moving situation will hold still. The app is a loop, not a pipeline — nothing here is ever "done."

The Self-Made Box

Untested assumptions

Repeating a guess until it feels true. A Blind-Spot Audit turns an AI into a hostile critic, hunting the blind spots you can't see.

The Rational High Ground

Claims you never grounded

Naming a Theme you "just felt." You can't name Evidence without two signals, or a Theme without examined Patterns.

Identification

Your slice = the whole

Mistaking your corner of the field for all of it. This tool refuses to do framing — forcing you to leave its identity behind.

The remedy

Earn every level

The locks aren't friction for its own sake — each one is a syndrome the app won't let you walk into.

The ladder · 04

You can't skip a rung.

Sensemaking here is a strict sequence. Each level is earned from the one below — and the app physically prevents you from naming a higher claim before its foundation is solid.

  1. Load raw field data — pour in everything your Pod observed, contradictions included.
  2. Sort it — fast binary decisions, signal or noise. Where you hesitate is where your instincts surface.
  3. Triangulate the survivors — two independent signals make Evidence; evidence makes Patterns; patterns make Themes.

The lock is the point. Try to name an Evidence card from one signal, or a Theme on an unexamined Pattern, and the field stays disabled — with a note telling you which syndrome you were about to walk into.

Sync, then hand off · 05

How a Pod merges minds.

This app runs locally in your browser — there's no server. The Pod syncs through Git, and that's deliberate.

  1. Export a .zip, unzip it into your local clone of the Pod's shared repository, then commit.
  2. Git merges everyone's maps — and where two of you mapped the same thing differently, the conflict makes you talk it out. That's how thirty people build one map without a Lone Warrior.
  3. Meet the Pods — your mapped Themes are what you present to the community. After that, you'll use them to generate Frames and propose solutions, in other tools.

This tool does the groundwork a frame has to stand on. It maps the situation and the human themes beneath it. The framing comes next — and it'll have something real to push against.

Map the Problem Situation

Your Pod collected raw observations from the field. Your mission over the next few weeks: figure out what's really going on underneath them, back it up with evidence, and map the Problem Situation so your Themes can survive scrutiny at Meet the Pods.

The canvas enforces Kees Dorst's Frame Creation method, made physical: every level is earned from the one below. The app won't let you name a Theme until the evidence beneath it is solid — that's how it keeps you off The Rational High Ground (defending claims you never grounded), out of The Self-Made Box (repeating untested assumptions), and away from the Lone Warrior's private certainty. This isn't bureaucracy; it's how expert designers reason.

  1. Connect the dots. Drag related observations together and link them. Two signals pointing at the same condition make an Evidence card.
  2. Build up. Link Evidence into a Pattern — a named mechanism. Link Patterns into a Theme — the deeper force that bridges human experience and structural conditions.
  3. Name every card. Say what you actually think is happening, not just a topic. Cards higher up stay locked until the ones below them are named & described.
  4. Commit. Once you have at least one Theme that's named, described, and passes the Bridge & Depth tests, hit Commit — synthesizing the whole ladder into a Problem Situation. Then you'll classify your evidence into Dorst's seven types and deepen the map.

The chip above the canvas shows how close you are.

Your working folder is downloaded — here's the Git handoff

This .zip is your complete evidence map — the semantic graph, the narrative Markdown, the AI-ready prompts, and a deployable static site. The tool has no server; your Pod syncs through one shared Git repo, and the manual loop is what keeps any one person from framing alone.

  1. Unzip the folder into your local clone of the Pod's shared repository (overwriting your previous version).
  2. Commit & push with three commands: 
    git add .
git commit -m "Update situation map — [your name]"
git push
  3. Git merges your map with your podmates'. A merge conflict isn't an error — it's a conversation: two of you saw the same thing differently, so talk it out, then commit the resolution.
  4. Publish when ready: push to a GitHub repository, then open Settings → Pages and pick the branch. Your mapped Problem Situation becomes a public knowledge graph at https://<user>.github.io/<repo>/.

This manual loop is intentional. It teaches enterprise version control and prevents the Lone Warrior syndrome — no single person's export overwrites everyone else's work without Git making you negotiate.

Combating The Self-Made Box

Adversarial Blind-Spot Audit

Dorst's Frame Creation requires you to have your own perceptions challenged before finalizing the map. This compiles your entire canvas into one hostile critique. Copy it, then paste into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, NotebookLM, or Perplexity.

Read the AI's response out loud to your Pod. If everyone agrees with the critique, you haven't pushed hard enough. Use it to find the signals you missed, the stakeholders you haven't reached, and the assumptions you've been hiding behind — then come back and revise your map. That's what the loop is for.
